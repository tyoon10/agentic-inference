#### **Announcements**

**1)** [OpenAI raises \$122 billion to accelerate the next phase of AI][1]
**Mar 31, 2026**
OpenAI closed its latest funding round at \$122 billion, valuing the company at \$852 billion. Proceeds will expand frontier AI globally, deepen ties with AWS, NVIDIA and SoftBank, and fund next-gen compute at scale. Consumer and enterprise momentum is accelerating—ChatGPT tops 900 million weekly active users, while enterprise revenue now exceeds \$2 billion per month and is on track to match consumer revenue by year-end 2026.


**2)** [Google DeepMind rolls out Veo 3.1 Lite and Lyria 3 Pro for creators][2]
**Mar 31, 2026**
Google released Veo 3.1 Lite, a cost-efficient video generation model (≤ 50 % of Veo 3.1 Fast) and Lyria 3 Pro, a music generator enabling longer, structure-aware tracks. Both are accessible via the Gemini API and Google AI Studio, with Veo 3.1 Fast pricing also dropping on Apr 7.


**3)** [NVIDIA & energy leaders launch power-flexible AI factories to stabilize the grid][3]
**Mar 31, 2026**
NVIDIA announced a new architecture that treats AI factories as grid assets, collaborating with AES, Constellation, NextEra, Vistra and others to harmonize compute load with real-time grid conditions. Objective: maximize tokens per second per watt while enhancing grid stability through dynamic demand response. Tokens-per-watt have already improved >1 million× since Kepler in 2012.


**4)** [IBM Granite 4.0 3B Vision released on Hugging Face Hub][4]
**Mar 31, 2026**
IBM open-sourced Granite 4.0 3B Vision, a compact multimodal model for enterprise documents. Combined with Hugging Face TRL v1.0 post-training library, teams can now fine-tune Granite variants rapidly with state-of-the-art post-training tooling.


**5)** [OpenAI partners with Gradient Labs to embed AI agents in banking][5]
**Apr 1, 2026**
Gradient Labs is deploying OpenAI’s GPT‑4.1 and new GPT‑5.4 mini/nano models to create AI account managers that handle fraud, payments and customer workflows with 97 % trajectory accuracy and <500 ms latency—even under voice workloads. Initial deployments cut queue time and raise customer satisfaction 98 %.


**6)** [OpenAI releases GPT‑5.4 mini and nano][6]
**Mar 17, 2026**
Small yet potent models arrive: GPT‑5.4 mini more than doubles speed vs GPT‑5 mini at similar cost and keeps GPT‑5.4-grade performance on SWE-Bench Pro; nano targets classification and sub-agent loops. Both are instrumented for low-latency agentic coding and screen-reading—critical for Codex and computer-use workflows.


**7)** [Hugging Face launches TRL v1.0 post-training library][7]
**Mar 31, 2026**
TRL v1.0 supports all major post-training workflows and is designed to evolve alongside the field. Coupled with Granite 4.0 3B Vision and HF Hub storage buckets, developers gain an end-to-end stack for enterprise-grade multimodal agents at lower cost.

#### **Insights & Analysis**

**8)** [AI benchmarks are broken—here’s what we need instead][8]
**Mar 31, 2026**
MIT Technology Review argues one-off tests fail real users because AI is deployed inside human teams and workflows over weeks, not seconds. Proposes HAIC benchmarks—Human–AI, Context-Specific Evaluation—that measure performance in messy, collaborative environments and across extended time horizons to avoid the dreaded “AI graveyard.”



**9)** [The gig workers training humanoid robots at home][9]
**Apr 1, 2026**
Teams in Nigeria, India and Argentina are filming laundry, dishes and chores for Micro1 and partners to train next-gen humanoid robots. Pay reaches \$15/hr locally, but raises questions on privacy, consent and the trade-offs of labeling massive swaths of human activity for physical-world AI.



**10)** [Self-organizing LLM agents outperform designed hierarchies][10]
**Mar 30, 2026**
A 25 000-task experiment shows giving capable models autonomy plus minimal scaffolding yields emergent role specialization 14 % better than centralized control and scales to 256 agents without quality loss. Open-source stacks achieve 95 % of closed-source quality at 24× lower cost.



**11)** [Pentagon’s attempt to blacklist Anthropic backfires][11]
**Mar 30, 2026**
A federal judge blocked the Pentagon from labeling Anthropic a supply-chain risk after officials “tweet first, lawyer later.” The ruling deemed the action punitive and lacking evidence, stirring broader unease over weaponized procurement and First-Amendment concerns.



**12)** [Reliability science: beyond pass@1 for long-horizon agents][12]
**Mar 31, 2026**
arXiv paper introduces four metrics—RDC, VAF, GDS, MOP—to quantify reliability decay, failure propagation and graceful degradation at long horizons. Key finding: frontier models achieve highest capability yet suffer up to 19 % meltdowns; memory scaffolds often hurt, not help, long-horizon performance. A call to move beyond single-attempt SWE-style snapshots.
